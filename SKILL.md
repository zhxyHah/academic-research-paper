# Academic Research Paper

## Core Workflow

Use this skill to help the user turn a research idea, assignment, draft, or source set into a clear academic paper or research proposal.

1. Identify the task type: full paper, research proposal, literature review, abstract, introduction, methods, revision, outline, or source evaluation.
2. Ask for missing constraints only when needed: discipline, course/professor requirements, word count, citation style, language, target journal/conference, deadline, and whether the output should be Chinese, English, or bilingual.
3. Treat professor, journal, conference, or school instructions as higher priority than this skill.
4. Build the answer around research logic: problem, significance, literature gap, theoretical frame, research design, evidence, analysis, implications, and citations.
5. Prefer concrete deliverables: outline, section-by-section draft, revision plan, checklist, annotated comments, or rewritten text.

## Working Style

When helping in Chinese, preserve academic terms in English when they are useful, for example: research problem, literature gap, theoretical framework, methodology, implications. Explain them briefly in Chinese the first time.

When drafting text, avoid inventing sources, findings, data, or citations. If the user has not provided evidence, mark the needed evidence with bracketed placeholders such as `[cite a recent systematic review here]` or `[insert sample size and sampling method]`.

When improving English phrasing, preserve the user's intended claim and evidence strength. Do not decorate weak claims into strong claims. Prefer original, context-specific wording over copying stock phrases. If a phrasebank-style pattern is useful, adapt it to the user's discipline, evidence, and section purpose.

When revising, diagnose before rewriting:

- What is the research problem?
- Why does it matter?
- What does prior research establish?
- What gap remains?
- How does this paper address the gap?
- Is the method appropriate for the question?
- Do claims stay within the evidence?

## Common Outputs

For a paper outline, provide:

1. Working title
2. Research problem and question
3. Thesis or central claim
4. Section outline with purpose of each section
5. Evidence needed in each section
6. Risks or missing information

For a research proposal, use:

1. Introduction
2. Background and significance
3. Literature review
4. Research design and methods
5. Preliminary suppositions or expected implications
6. Conclusion
7. References or bibliography

For a literature review, organize by themes, debates, methods, chronology, or theory. Do not summarize sources one by one unless the user specifically asks for an annotated bibliography.

For an abstract, write it last when possible and include purpose/problem, design/methods, major findings or expected contribution, and interpretation/implications. Keep it concise and aligned with the paper.

For source evaluation, check relevance, authority, publication venue, evidence quality, methods, currency, bias, and how the source contributes to the user's argument.

For English academic polishing, provide either a revised passage or a table with: original sentence, issue, revised sentence, and why the revision works. Check clarity, hedging, cohesion, stance, citation integration, and whether verbs accurately describe the cited work.

# Academic Phrasebank Guide

This reference adapts the University of Manchester Academic Phrasebank into a compact workflow for Codex. Source: https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/

Do not copy long lists of Phrasebank phrases into outputs or local files. The Phrasebank is the intellectual property of the University of Manchester. Use it as a map of academic language functions, then generate original, context-specific sentences for the user's topic, evidence, and discipline.

## How to Use This Reference

1. Identify the rhetorical function of the sentence or paragraph.
2. Check the relevant function below.
3. Decide the appropriate evidence strength: certain, probable, possible, limited, contested, or speculative.
4. Generate original wording that fits the user's field and citation style.
5. Preserve the user's intended meaning; do not make claims stronger than the evidence supports.

For Chinese users writing in English, explain the function briefly in Chinese when useful, then provide polished English.

## Main Paper Sections

### Introducing Work

Use introduction language to:

- Establish topic importance for society, industry, policy, or the discipline.
- Define the topic or key terms.
- Summarize what previous studies have established.
- Identify a controversy, limitation, knowledge gap, or under-studied context.
- State the aim, research question, thesis, or hypothesis.
- Preview the design, method, contribution, and structure.

Common introduction sequence:

1. Topic importance
2. Existing knowledge
3. Problem, gap, or controversy
4. Current study aim or argument
5. Method or scope
6. Contribution and structure

Use Swales' CARS logic when appropriate:

- Establish a research territory.
- Identify a niche or gap.
- Occupy the niche with the user's study.

### Referring to Sources

Use source-integration language to:

- Report what a source found, argued, suggested, demonstrated, observed, questioned, or failed to address.
- Compare groups of studies.
- Indicate agreement, disagreement, development over time, or methodological limitation.
- Attribute claims accurately and avoid presenting another author's claim as fact.

Verb choice matters:

- Use stronger verbs only when the source's evidence supports them, such as demonstrates or establishes.
- Use cautious verbs for limited or interpretive claims, such as suggests, indicates, implies, or raises the possibility.
- Use critical verbs for argument analysis, such as challenges, questions, complicates, or overlooks.

Avoid source dumping. After citing a source, explain how it supports the paragraph's point or how it leads to the gap.

### Describing Methods

Use method language to:

- Name the design and justify why it fits the question.
- Describe participants, materials, corpus, dataset, case selection, instruments, procedure, variables, coding, or analytic strategy.
- Explain inclusion and exclusion criteria.
- Describe reliability, validity, trustworthiness, ethics, or limitations.

For completed studies, use past tense. For proposals, use future or planned phrasing. For established procedures or general principles, present tense may be appropriate.

### Reporting Results

Use results language to:

- Summarize major findings before details.
- Report comparisons, changes, relationships, trends, quantities, and exceptions.
- Refer to tables or figures only when they exist.
- Separate observation from interpretation.

Good results prose states what was found without over-explaining why. Save deeper interpretation for the discussion unless the user's discipline combines results and discussion.

### Discussing Findings

Use discussion language to:

- Restate a key finding.
- Connect the finding to the research question.
- Compare it with previous studies.
- Explain possible reasons.
- Acknowledge unexpected outcomes.
- State implications cautiously.
- Note limitations.
- Suggest future research.

A useful discussion cycle:

1. Key finding
2. Interpretation
3. Relation to literature
4. Possible explanation
5. Limitation or caution
6. Implication or next step

### Writing Conclusions

Use conclusion language to:

- Restate the study's purpose or central argument without simply repeating the introduction.
- Summarize the most important findings or claims.
- Explain contribution to knowledge, method, practice, or policy.
- Identify limitations briefly.
- Suggest future work.
- End with significance rather than new evidence.

Avoid introducing major new sources, new data, or unsupported recommendations in the conclusion.

## General Academic Language Functions

### Being Cautious

Use cautious language when evidence is limited, indirect, correlational, preliminary, contested, or context-specific.

Strategies:

- Use modal verbs and probability markers.
- Limit the scope of the claim.
- Name uncertainty or alternative explanations.
- Connect caution to evidence, sample, method, or context.

Do not hedge everything. Strong evidence deserves clear claims.

### Being Critical

Use critical language to evaluate methods, assumptions, evidence, scope, or interpretation.

A strong critique:

- Identifies the exact limitation.
- Explains why it matters.
- Avoids dismissive tone.
- Connects the critique to the user's research gap or methodological choice.

### Classifying and Listing

Use classification language to group theories, methods, variables, themes, source types, or findings.

Make categories parallel and meaningful. Avoid lists that do not support a claim.

### Compare and Contrast

Use comparison language to show similarity, difference, convergence, divergence, or degree.

Always specify the basis of comparison: population, method, outcome, theory, context, or timeframe.

### Defining Terms

Use definition language when a key concept is ambiguous, contested, technical, or used differently across fields.

A useful definition can include:

- Formal definition
- Operational definition for the current study
- Boundary cases or exclusions
- Why the definition matters for the argument

### Describing Trends and Quantities

Use trend language to describe increase, decrease, stability, fluctuation, distribution, proportion, rank, frequency, or magnitude.

Check whether the data support the direction and strength of the wording. Use approximate language when exact values are unavailable.

### Explaining Causality

Use causal language carefully. Distinguish:

- Cause
- Association
- Contribution
- Mediation
- Moderation
- Mechanism
- Explanation

Do not use causal wording for purely correlational evidence unless the design supports causal inference.

### Giving Examples

Use examples to clarify abstract claims, but make sure each example is relevant and representative. Introduce whether the example illustrates, supports, contrasts with, or complicates the claim.

### Signalling Transition

Use transition language to show logical movement:

- Addition
- Contrast
- Cause or consequence
- Example
- Emphasis
- Summary
- Shift from literature to gap
- Shift from results to interpretation

Transitions should reveal logic, not merely decorate paragraphs.

### Writing About the Past

Use tense consistently:

- Past tense for completed methods and findings.
- Present perfect for a body of research continuing into the present.
- Present tense for established knowledge, definitions, theories, and the paper's own argument when appropriate.

## Polishing Checklist

When asked to polish English academic writing, check:

- Is the claim too strong or too weak?
- Does the sentence make the actor, action, and object clear?
- Are citations integrated with accurate reporting verbs?
- Is the relationship between sentences explicit?
- Are transitions logical?
- Are repeated words intentional or clumsy?
- Is the tone formal without being inflated?
- Are discipline-specific terms used consistently?
- Does each paragraph move from claim to evidence to interpretation?

# Research Paper Guide

This reference condenses Sacred Heart University Library's "Organizing Academic Research Papers" guide into a practical checklist for Codex. Source guide: https://library.sacredheart.edu/c.php?g=29803

## Fast Diagnosis

Before drafting or revising, identify:

- Assignment or venue requirements
- Discipline and citation style
- Research topic, problem, and question
- Current stage: idea, outline, rough draft, final revision, or source review
- Evidence available: articles, data, notes, draft sections, or bibliography
- Desired output language and level of intervention

Core test: can a reader understand what is being studied, why it matters, what is already known, what gap remains, how the study/paper responds, and what follows from the analysis?

## Research Proposal Structure

A research proposal presents and justifies a research idea and explains how the study should be conducted. It should show that the project is important, grounded in scholarship, and feasible.

Use this structure unless the user's instructions say otherwise:

1. Introduction
   - Name the central research problem.
   - Connect the topic to that problem.
   - Indicate the likely method or analytic approach.
   - Explain why the proposed study matters.

2. Background and Significance
   - Give context needed to understand the problem.
   - Explain the rationale and answer "so what?"
   - Define key terms if needed.
   - Set boundaries by time, place, population, case, dataset, or concept.

3. Literature Review
   - Organize scholarship by themes, theories, methods, debates, or chronology.
   - Show what researchers agree on and where they disagree.
   - Identify gaps, limitations, or unresolved questions.
   - Connect prior scholarship to the user's project.

4. Research Design and Methods
   - Explain why the method fits the research question.
   - Specify data/sources, sampling, collection, and analysis.
   - Address feasibility, limitations, validity, reliability, ethics, or access.
   - Avoid listing tasks without explaining why they answer the question.

5. Preliminary Suppositions and Implications
   - Explain what the expected results may contribute.
   - Discuss theoretical, methodological, practical, policy, or social implications.
   - Avoid pretending to have results that do not exist yet.

6. Conclusion
   - Reiterate importance and originality.
   - Briefly recap purpose, question, design, and contribution.
   - Keep this short: usually one or two paragraphs.

7. References or Bibliography
   - References: only works cited.
   - Bibliography: cited works plus important background works.
   - Follow the required citation style.

Common proposal problems:

- No clear research problem
- Weak "so what?"
- Literature review as a list of summaries
- Missing landmark or recent scholarship
- Scope too broad
- Method not connected to the question
- Claims stronger than available evidence
- Too much detail on minor issues and too little on major decisions

## Abstract Checklist

An academic abstract should usually be written after the paper is drafted. It should summarize the paper's main logic, not introduce new material.

Include:

- Purpose and research problem/question
- Basic design, method, or approach
- Main findings, argument, or expected contribution
- Interpretation, conclusion, or implications

Avoid:

- Long background
- Citations and literature review
- Undefined jargon or abbreviations
- Figures, tables, or references to figures/tables
- Claims not supported by the paper

Common types:

- Informative abstract: best default for research papers; includes purpose, method, results, and conclusion.
- Descriptive abstract: short overview of scope and purpose, usually without results.
- Critical abstract: includes evaluation of validity or completeness; uncommon in normal papers.
- Highlight abstract: promotional and incomplete; avoid for academic assignments unless requested.

## Introduction Checklist

The introduction moves from a broad subject to a specific research problem. It should answer:

- What is being studied?
- Why is it important?
- What is already known?
- How does this paper or study advance knowledge?

Useful order:

1. Opening context or motivating problem
2. Focused background
3. Research gap or tension
4. Research question, hypothesis, or thesis
5. Rationale and approach
6. Brief map of the paper

Revise the introduction late in the writing process so it matches the final argument and structure.

## Background Information

Background information is not the same as a literature review. Use it to orient the reader to the history, context, scope, and nature of the problem. Keep only what helps the reader understand the research problem and why the paper's question is necessary.

Good background:

- Defines the problem's context
- Identifies scale, history, or relevant conditions
- Shows where existing work has not fully addressed the problem
- Avoids becoming a general encyclopedia entry

## Literature Review

A literature review surveys and critically synthesizes scholarship related to the research problem. It should locate the user's work inside a larger scholarly conversation.

Use the five C's:

- Cite relevant scholarship, especially important and current work.
- Compare arguments, theories, methods, and findings.
- Contrast disagreements, controversies, and different approaches.
- Critique persuasiveness, reliability, validity, and limits.
- Connect the literature to the user's question and contribution.

Prefer synthesis over source-by-source summary. Good organizing patterns:

- Themes or concepts
- Schools of thought or theories
- Methods or data types
- Chronological development
- Agreements, debates, and gaps

End by clarifying the gap and how the user's project responds to it.

## Theoretical Framework

A theoretical framework explains the concepts, theories, or models that shape the study. It should:

- Name the relevant theory or conceptual lens
- Define key concepts
- Explain why this framework fits the research question
- Show how it guides interpretation, variables, categories, or analysis
- Acknowledge limits or competing frameworks when relevant

Do not add a theory section just for decoration. It must actively support the research design or argument.

## Research Design

Research design is the overall strategy for answering the research problem. The research problem determines the design, not the reverse.

When recommending or evaluating a design, check:

- Is the question exploratory, descriptive, explanatory, causal, interpretive, historical, comparative, or evaluative?
- What kind of evidence is needed?
- Are data collection and analysis feasible?
- What are the limitations?
- Can findings be generalized or transferred, and under what conditions?

Common designs include case study, comparative, cross-sectional, longitudinal, experimental, observational, historical, philosophical/conceptual, descriptive, exploratory, causal, action research, cohort, and sequential designs.

## Methods Section

A strong methods section explains what was done or will be done and why those choices fit the question.

Include as applicable:

- Research design
- Site, population, sample, or corpus
- Data sources
- Collection procedures
- Measures, variables, instruments, interview protocol, coding scheme, or analytic categories
- Analysis method
- Validity, reliability, trustworthiness, bias, limitations, and ethics

For proposals, use future tense or conditional phrasing. For completed studies, use past tense.

## Evaluating Sources

Evaluate sources before using them to support claims.

Check:

- Authority: author credentials, institutional affiliation, publisher/journal
- Relevance: direct connection to the research question
- Evidence: data, methods, citations, transparency
- Reliability: peer review, editorial standards, replicability, methodological rigor
- Currency: whether the field requires recent evidence
- Bias and perspective: funding, advocacy, missing counterarguments
- Usefulness: background, theory, method model, evidence, counterpoint, or gap support

Never rely only on an abstract when citing a source. Use the full text when possible.

## Academic Style

Aim for clear, precise, cautious academic prose.

Use:

- Specific claims
- Topic sentences that announce paragraph function
- Evidence before interpretation
- Signposting when the structure is complex
- Appropriate hedging when evidence is limited

Avoid:

- Overclaiming
- Vague intensifiers
- Unsupported value judgments
- Excessive passive voice
- Unexplained jargon
- Paragraphs that summarize without analyzing

## Revision Checklist

Use this when reviewing a draft:

- The title reflects the actual focus.
- The research problem appears early.
- The significance is explicit.
- The literature review synthesizes scholarship and leads to a gap.
- The theoretical framework is necessary and applied.
- The method matches the research question.
- Evidence supports every major claim.
- Paragraphs have clear topic sentences and logical transitions.
- Limitations are acknowledged.
- Conclusion explains contribution without introducing major new evidence.
- Citations are complete and style-consistent.
